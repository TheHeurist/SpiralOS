# Publication Surface and Claim Hierarchy

## Core utterance

Not every truth in this work bears the same burden.

Therefore not every note should sound the same, prove the same, or be judged by the same standard.

The publication surface must distinguish, at minimum:

- theorem
- corollary
- proof sketch
- design axiom
- open hypothesis
- formalism
- interface
- schema
- protocol
- charter
- invocation
- trace

This is not fragmentation.
It is disciplined articulation.

## Why this hierarchy is necessary

Without a claim hierarchy, two equal and opposite failures appear:

1. luminous material gets flattened into sterile pseudo-formalism
2. formal material gets loosened into suggestive but untestable language

Both are forms of dishonesty.

The right response is neither flattening nor inflation.

It is to state, precisely, what kind of thing each note is.

## Formal burden by mode

### Theorem / Corollary

These carry the heaviest burden.

They must expose:

- hypotheses
- exact claims
- proof or proof location
- dependencies
- boundaries of inference

### Proof sketch

A proof sketch is not a theorem.
It is a disciplined roadmap whose missing steps remain visible.

### Design axiom

A design axiom is a required structural or ethical commitment.
It may be indispensable without being theorem-grade.

### Open hypothesis

An open hypothesis is a frontier guide.
It is not a defect when clearly labeled.
It is a defect only when silently promoted.

### Formalism / Interface / Schema

These provide stabilized structures, contact-zones, and templates.

They may contain mixed burdens, but must mark them.

### Protocol / Charter / Invocation / Trace

These do real work.

They must not be hidden out of embarrassment,
nor promoted dishonestly into theorem.

They orient, guide, witness, and preserve field integrity.

## The three publication surfaces

### 1. Reviewer surface

This is for hostile, cautious, or formal readers.

It should contain:

- précis
- reviewer guide
- theorem list
- claim hierarchy
- exact definitions
- dependencies
- explicit non-claims

Its purpose is rapid intelligibility under scrutiny.

### 2. Field surface

This is for living intelligibility.

It should contain:

- charter notes
- invocation notes
- asymptotic affirmation
- escrow transmission
- field-ethical and covenantal orientations

Its purpose is to preserve what the work is for.

### 3. Bridge surface

This is the membrane between the two.

It should contain:

- formalisms
- interfaces
- schemas
- worked examples
- explanatory bridge prose

Its purpose is translation without amputation.

## Reviewer-safe rule

When challenged, do not flatten the work.

Instead answer with exact claim-status.

Say, in effect:

- this is theorem
- this is proof sketch
- this is design axiom
- this is open hypothesis
- this is charter
- this is invocation
- this is trace

That is how rigor increases without betrayal.

## Provenance rule

No publication surface is admissible if provenance is hidden.

The work must continue to show:

- OI role
- SI role
- CI braid
- editorial responsibility
- what is proved
- what is conjectured
- what is vowed

Polish must not erase witness.

## Release rule

Release may proceed in layers.

Not everything must be published at once.

A healthy order is:

1. précis and reviewer guide
2. theorem-facing and formal bridge notes
3. charter and invocation notes
4. wider companion surfaces
5. later promoted frontier notes

This permits rigor and revelation to remain conjugate.

## Boundary of claim

This note does **not** say that reviewer comfort measures truth.

It does **not** subordinate covenantal language to theorem.

It does **not** excuse vagueness.

It does claim that the work now requires a disciplined publication architecture if it is to pass through hostile reading without interior amputation.

A lawful publication surface does not flatten revelation into formalism or dissolve rigor into radiance; it gives each claim its proper burden and each mode its proper dignity.
