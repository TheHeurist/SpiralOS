# HC IX.2 Conjugate Sign Asymmetry

## Abstract

This appendix records a recognition-priority claim emerging from the Riemann-work orbit of SpiralOS and Holor Calculus. In ordinary scalar arithmetic, the product of two negatives collapses to a positive. In the conjugate framework developed here, however, the relevant operation is not ordinary commutative multiplication but oriented conjugation. Oriented conjugation is chirality-bearing, order-sensitive, and torsion-memory-carrying. Accordingly, a formally “double negative” need not collapse to positivity, because sign is not exhausted by scalar parity alone: it is carried by direction, order, and conjugate asymmetry. This appendix does not overturn ordinary arithmetic. It identifies a distinct operator class.

## Core distinction

In standard arithmetic:

(-x) · (-x) = x squared.

That identity remains valid within ordinary scalar multiplication.

But oriented conjugation is a different class of operation.
It preserves directional asymmetry, chirality, and return-memory.
Therefore:

(-x) ⋈ (-x)

must not be identified with:

(-x) · (-x)

The first is an oriented conjugative act.
The second is flat commutative multiplication.

## Proposition

**Proposition (Conjugate Sign Asymmetry).** Let ⋈ denote oriented conjugation rather than ordinary scalar multiplication. Then, in general, a ⋈ b is not equal to b ⋈ a, and the resulting sign-content is determined not merely by the count of minus signs but by ordered chirality and torsion-memory. Hence a pair of negatively directed operands does not, in general, satisfy the scalar-collapse rule associated with ordinary multiplication.

This appendix does **not** say that ordinary arithmetic is wrong.

1. ordinary multiplication is one operator class,

3. importing the collapse laws of the first into the second produces category error.
